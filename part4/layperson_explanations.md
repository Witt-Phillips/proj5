# Part 4 -- Layperson Explanations

### D1 -- Maya, 9-year-old biological child

**Is Maya a dependent? Yes.**

Maya counts as her mother Jane's dependent for the tax year. She's Jane's own biological child, she lived in Jane's house the whole year, and at nine years old she's clearly young enough. She doesn't have a job or savings of her own, so Jane was the one paying for everything. Maya hasn't gotten married or filed a tax return with a spouse, and she's a U.S. citizen. All the boxes the tax code wants checked for a "child" dependent are checked, so Jane can claim her.

---

### D2 -- Jacob, 21-year-old full-time student

**Is Jacob a dependent? Yes.**

Jacob still counts as his father Ravi's dependent even though he's 21. The tax code gives a break for full-time college students: as long as a child is under 24 and in school full-time, they can still be claimed as a dependent child. Jacob is in that window. His parents' home is still his main address, he earned only a small amount from a summer job, and his dad is paying for most of his life (tuition, housing, insurance). He isn't married, and he's a U.S. citizen. That adds up to a yes.

---

### D3 -- Elena, 72-year-old resident mother

**Is Elena a dependent? Yes.**

Elena, who is 72, can be claimed as a dependent by her son Marcos. She doesn't fit the "child" rules, but there's a separate set of rules for supporting adult relatives. Elena is Marcos's mother (parents and other close relatives qualify), she has a green card so she counts as a U.S. resident, and she made only a small amount of money in the year -- well under the cut-off the law uses. Marcos is paying for most of her housing, food, and medical costs. She also isn't being claimed as anyone else's "child dependent." That means Marcos can claim her.

---

### N1 -- Sarah, 17-year-old who filed a joint return

**Is Sarah a dependent? No.**

On paper Sarah looks just like the kind of teenager a parent could claim: she's 17, she lives at home with Priya, she didn't earn a dime, and Priya paid for everything. But Sarah got married during the year and she and her husband filed a joint tax return together to report his wages and actually settle their tax bill. The rule here is strict: if someone files a joint return with a spouse for the year (and it isn't purely a technicality done only to get a refund of withheld tax), they can't be claimed as anyone's dependent, full stop. That one fact disqualifies her.

### N2 -- Marcus, 22-year-old self-supporting son

**Is Marcus a dependent? No.**

Marcus can't be claimed by his father Dmitri. The "child dependent" route doesn't work: Marcus is 22, he isn't a student, and he isn't disabled, so he's past the age the law allows. It also wouldn't work even if he were younger, because he paid for most of his own life out of his $41,000 job. The "adult relative" route also fails: to claim an adult relative you need their yearly income to be under a fairly small number (about $5,000 for this year), and you need to have paid for more than half of their support. Marcus blew past the income limit and his father isn't covering most of his costs. Either way, he's financially on his own.

---

### N3 -- Lin, friend / tenant

**Is Lin a dependent? No.**

Noah can't claim Lin as a dependent. Lin is a close friend -- not Noah's child, grandchild, sibling, parent, aunt, uncle, niece, nephew, in-law, or any of the other family relationships the tax code lists. The one catch-all that could help -- "someone who lives with you as part of your household for the whole year" -- doesn't fit either, because Lin is really a tenant renting the basement, not a member of the household. She also earns far too much on her own ($38,000) to qualify as anyone's adult-relative dependent. So the answer is no: she's a friend, and friendships don't make tax dependents.
