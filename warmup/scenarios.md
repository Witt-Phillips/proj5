# Warm-up: Six Scenarios for 26 U.S.C. 152 (Dependent Definition)

Per the Project 5 warm-up instructions, these six scenarios were generated by an LLM. For this submission the "LLM" is the Cursor assistant (Anthropic Claude, used here as a free-of-charge model per the assignment's cost restriction). The prompts used to elicit them are transcribed at the bottom of this file.

Three scenarios are intended to resolve to **dependent** (compliant) under the statute, and three are intended to resolve to **not a dependent** (non-compliant). Each is written with enough factual richness that legal compliance is a non-trivial question. The corresponding SMT-LIB encodings live in `../part2/scenario_<slug>.smt2`.

## Dependent scenarios

### Scenario D1 -- "Maya, the young biological child" (`dep_child`)

Maya is 9 years old and is the biological daughter of her taxpayer mother, Jane. Throughout the 2024 tax year Maya lived exclusively in Jane's house in Chicago, which is also Jane's principal place of abode. Maya is a U.S. citizen by birth. She is not married and has filed no return of any kind. Maya does not work and has no savings; Jane covers 100% of her food, shelter, clothing, medical care, and schooling. Jane's household is compliant with all applicable local laws.

**Intended classification:** Dependent via the **qualifying child** path (every 152(c)(1) clause met, and 152(b)(2) and (b)(3) satisfied).

### Scenario D2 -- "Jacob, the college-student son" (`dep_student`)

Jacob is a 21-year-old undergraduate attending a public university 250 miles from his parents' home. He is the biological son of his taxpayer father, Ravi. Jacob is a full-time student (enrolled in 15 credit hours each of the spring and fall semesters). He maintains his parents' address as his principal place of abode; during winter and summer breaks (roughly six months total including the periods he is enrolled and deemed a student) he lives in Ravi's home. He is a U.S. citizen, unmarried, did not file a joint return. Jacob earned $4,800 from a summer internship; Ravi provides the other approximately 82% of his support (tuition, room/board, health insurance). Ravi's household is compliant with local law.

**Intended classification:** Dependent via the **qualifying child** path, using the 152(c)(3)(A)(ii) full-time-student extension through age 24.

### Scenario D3 -- "Elena, the elderly mother" (`dep_mother`)

Elena is 72 years old and is the biological mother of her taxpayer son, Marcos. Elena is a lawful permanent resident of the United States (holds a green card) and lives full-time in Marcos's home in Miami. Elena's only 2024 income is $3,000 of interest from a small savings account; her Social Security benefits are excluded from gross income. The 2024 exemption amount is $5,050. Marcos pays for Elena's housing, food, utilities, and prescription drugs, covering approximately 80% of her total support. Elena is not a qualifying child of any taxpayer. She did not file a joint return. Nothing about the living arrangement violates local law.

**Intended classification:** Dependent via the **qualifying relative** path (152(d) clauses (A)-(D) all satisfied; the QR relationship is satisfied by 152(d)(2)(C) "father, mother, or an ancestor of either").

## Non-dependent scenarios

### Scenario N1 -- "Sarah, the married daughter who filed jointly" (`nondep_joint`)

Sarah is 17 years old and is the biological daughter of her taxpayer mother, Priya. Sarah lived in Priya's home for the entire tax year and is a U.S. citizen. Priya provided 100% of Sarah's support. In June, Sarah married Tom (18). For the 2024 tax year Sarah and Tom filed a joint return on which they reported Tom's $52,000 in W-2 wages and paid $4,100 in federal income tax. They did not file the joint return solely to claim a refund of withholding -- they had a real tax liability and settled it.

**Intended classification:** Not a dependent. Under 152(b)(2), filing a joint return disqualifies Sarah entirely (and 152(c)(1)(E) independently would disqualify her from the qualifying-child path since the refund-only exception does not apply).

### Scenario N2 -- "Marcus, the self-supporting adult son" (`nondep_income`)

Marcus is 22 years old, the biological son of his taxpayer father, Dmitri, and lives in Dmitri's guest house. Marcus is not enrolled in school at all during 2024 (he dropped out last year) and is not disabled. He works full-time as a warehouse lead and earned $41,000 in W-2 wages, of which he used roughly 70% toward his own rent, food, insurance, and car payments -- more than half of his own support. He did not file a joint return. He is a U.S. citizen. The 2024 exemption amount is $5,050.

**Intended classification:** Not a dependent. Fails 152(c)(1)(C) (age test: over 19, not a student, not disabled) and 152(c)(1)(D) (provided over half of own support). Also fails 152(d)(1)(B) because gross income far exceeds the exemption amount.

### Scenario N3 -- "Lin, the friend who rents a room" (`nondep_friend`)

Lin is 34 years old and is a longtime friend of taxpayer Noah. Lin is a U.S. citizen. Starting in 2023, Lin rents the finished basement of Noah's house at below-market rent. Lin maintains her own kitchen, receives her own mail, files her own taxes, and is financially independent -- she is not a member of Noah's household, just a tenant. Lin is not Noah's child, sibling, parent, in-law, or any other enumerated relative in 152(d)(2)(A)-(G). Lin earned $38,000 as a graphic designer in 2024. She did not file a joint return. The tenancy complies with local zoning.

**Intended classification:** Not a dependent. Fails the QR relationship test 152(d)(1)(A) because she is in none of (A)-(G) and, because she is not a member of Noah's household, the 152(d)(2)(H) catch-all also does not apply. (She independently fails 152(d)(1)(B) on gross income.) She cannot be a qualifying child either because 152(c)(1)(A) / (2) require a child, sibling, or descendant-of-one.
